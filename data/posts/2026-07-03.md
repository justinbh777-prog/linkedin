## VARIANT A — Financial Hook

A 34-unit Club Pilates operator just got bought by private equity.

Two smaller franchisees got scooped up in the same deal. One had six units. One had three.

Here's what that tells you.

Buyers are getting picky. They're no longer paying top dollar for every fitness business that walks in the door.

The gap between an A-grade business and a C-grade one is growing fast.

What separates them? Three things. Room to grow. Steady numbers. And a management team that runs without you.

For a small owner, this matters more than you think.

As someone who works with franchise buyers and sellers every day, I can tell you the buyer's first question is rarely about revenue.

It's about you. Specifically, can this business run when you leave?

If the answer is no, your multiple drops. Every time.

The owners who sell high build a team years before they exit. Not months.

Three things to watch:

- Whether your business can run without you daily
- Open territory you still have room to grow into
- Consistent, provable numbers buyers can trust

## VARIANT B — Contrarian Hook

Two fitness businesses can look identical on paper and sell for wildly different prices.

Same unit count. Same revenue. Different buyer.

Why? Because buyers are now sorting deals into A, B, and C piles.

The A and B assets still trade at strong numbers. The C assets get punished.

What moves you up the ladder? Stability, room to grow, and a management team that runs without you.

Here's what this means if you own 3, 6, or even 20 units.

Buyers are digging deeper than they used to. They want to see clean growth rights, easy integration, and a team that does not depend on you showing up every day.

As someone who works with franchise buyers and sellers every day, the biggest value gap I see is the owner who IS the business.

If your locations only run because you are there, you are a C asset.

Build a business that runs without you. That is what turns a good multiple into a great one.

Three things to watch:

- Whether you have open territory left to grow into
- How dependent daily operations are on you
- The strength of your bench, not just your top line

# POST 2 — Personal Post

**Topic:** The conversation every franchise owner should have 3 years before selling

---

Most owners start planning their exit way too late.

By the time they call me, they want to sell in six months. That's not enough time.

The best conversation happens three years out. Here's why.

When you sell, buyers look at your last three years of numbers. Not one good year. Three.

So if you clean up your books, cut wasteful spending, and grow your profit starting now, you get credit for all of it at sale time.

I worked with an owner who called me two years before he wanted out. We spent that time fixing his financials and reducing how much the business depended on him.

His sale price came in almost 40% higher than his first estimate.

Compare that to owners who wait. They sell tired, in a rush, and leave real money on the table.

The takeaway is simple. Selling is not a decision. It's a process. And the best time to start is before you're ready.

Even a quick chat now can shape what you walk away with later.
