What they cared about most wasn't just the number of studios.

It was the management team. And the room left to grow.

Buyers are getting picky. They now separate the good deals from the great ones, and they pay up for the great ones.

# POST 2 — Personal Post

**Topic:** The valuation mistake I see franchise owners make over and over

---

Most franchise owners think their business is worth more than it is.

Not because they are wrong about the work they put in. But because they are valuing the wrong thing.

Here is what I mean.

Owners look at revenue. Buyers look at what is left after the owner walks away.

If your business only runs because you are there every day, that is a problem. A buyer has to replace you, and that costs money. So they pay less.

Last year I worked with an owner doing strong sales. Great numbers on paper. But he was the manager, the closer, and the face of the business. Take him out and the whole thing wobbled.

His valuation came in lower than he expected. Not because the business was bad. Because it depended on him.

The fix is simple, but it takes time.

Build a team. Document your systems. Make yourself replaceable before you sell.

The best time to do this is two or three years before you exit. Not two or three months.
