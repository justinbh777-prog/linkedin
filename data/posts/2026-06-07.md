$1 billion. For a franchisee — not a franchisor.

Last summer, Bain Capital acquired Sizzling Platter from CapitalSpring — an eight-brand operator group — at a valuation that broke records for an operator transaction.

This wasn't a franchisor buyout. This was PE paying a premium for an operating company.

The reason? Free cash flow generation and a proven history of growth across multiple brands.

Strong unit economics. Diversified royalty exposure. Predictable EBITDA.

That's what buyers paid 10 figures for.

Here's the catch — with eight brands came eight franchisors to negotiate with. The deal took 10 months to close.

Approval wasn't sequential. It was a multi-prong negotiation with interdependent terms across every franchisor at once.

As someone who works with franchise buyers and sellers every day, this is the part most operators underestimate.

You don't just sell your business. You sell it subject to your franchisor's approval — and transferability is a valuation driver most owners never price in.

The financial community wants cash-flowing operators with growth history. But the operator who structures clean financials and pre-clears franchisor consent commands a higher multiple — and closes faster.

Enterprise value gets created in the financials. It gets protected in the deal mechanics.

Three things to watch:

- PE appetite for multi-unit operators — not just brands — is accelerating consolidation
- Franchisor approval timelines are becoming the gating factor on close, not price
- Diversified brand portfolios are now commanding platform-level multiples

## POST VARIANT B — Contrarian Hook

Private equity isn't buying brands anymore. They're buying operators.

Last summer, Bain Capital acquired Sizzling Platter — an eight-brand franchisee group — from CapitalSpring for $1 billion.

Not a franchisor. A franchisee.

That's an unprecedented price tag for a PE firm buying an operator — and a signal of where the money is moving.

Why? Free cash flow generation and a proven history of growth across multiple brands.

The deal took 10 months to close. Eight brands meant eight franchisors — eight separate approvals — negotiated simultaneously, with interdependent terms.

Here's the pattern most owners miss.

Buyers no longer need to own the franchisor to capture franchise economics. A multi-brand operator with strong unit economics and durable EBITDA is now a platform acquisition in its own right.

That shifts the valuation conversation. The acquisition multiple follows free cash flow and same-store sales consistency — not logo prestige.

As someone who works with franchise buyers and sellers every day, I'll tell you what the headline number hides.

The $1 billion wasn't the hard part. The franchisor approval process was — transferability is where deals die.

If your enterprise value depends on a buyer getting approved by your franchisor, that approval risk is priced into your number today, whether you realize it or not.

Three things to watch:

- PE appetite for multi-brand operators — not just franchisors
- Franchisor approval timelines as a deal-killer on transferability
- Free cash flow becoming the primary valuation driver over brand name
