That same firm also scooped up a 6-unit and a 3-unit operator in the same category.

Different sizes. Same buyer. That tells you something.

Fitness valuations have cooled from the post-pandemic highs. But stable does not mean weak.

What changed is how buyers grade the deals.

The spread between A, B and C assets is wider than it used to be. Buyers are digging into management depth, growth rights, and how cleanly a business integrates.

For the 1-20 unit owner, this is the whole game.

You may not be a 34-unit operator. But that 3-unit deal in the same transaction proves smaller operators are still getting bought.

The question is which grade you fall into.

Buyers no longer pay up for revenue alone. They pay for a business that runs without you in the room.

The owners getting strong numbers built a team and clean books before they ever picked up the phone.

Three things to watch:

- Whether your same-store sales are holding or sliding

## POST VARIANT B — Contrarian Hook

*(Alternative — test this against Variant A)*

---

Two fitness franchises with identical revenue just sold for very different prices.

The difference wasn't location or unit count.

It was the management team.

Eagle Merchant Partners recently re-entered fitness, backing a 34-unit Club Pilates operator alongside a six-unit and a three-unit franchisee.

Their reasoning is worth your attention.

Buyers have gotten more discerning since the post-pandemic highs. The gap between A, B, and C assets is widening.

But here's the part most owners miss.

A-assets and B-assets are trading at roughly the same multiple. The penalty lands hardest on the C-assets - the ones that can't run without the owner.

For a 1-20 unit owner, this is the whole game.

If your business stalls the day you step away, you're a C-asset. No management bench means no transferability, and no transferability means a lower acquisition multiple.

As someone who works with franchise buyers and sellers every day, I'll tell you what closes the gap.

Buyers pay up for stability and white space. They want to know there's room to grow through de novo or acquisition, and a team in place to execute it.

The owners who get top dollar built their second-in-command years before they sold. The ones who waited got discounted at the table.

You can't manufacture a strong management team 60 days before a sale.

Three things to watch:

- Whether your business runs without you for two weeks straight
- Whether your market still has white space buyers can grow into
- Whether your same-store sales are stable, not just peaking
