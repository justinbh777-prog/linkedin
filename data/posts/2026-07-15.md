## VARIANT A — Financial Hook

A 34-unit Club Pilates operator just got bought by a private equity firm.

They didn't stop there. They also scooped up a 6-unit and a 3-unit operator in the same deal.

This is how consolidation works. Big buyers grab a strong anchor, then bolt on smaller shops around it.

Here's what caught my eye. Buyers are getting pickier.

They're paying up for what they call "A" and "B" assets. Clean books. Strong management team. Room to grow.

The weaker "C" businesses are falling behind on price.

If you own 1 to 20 fitness units, that gap matters to you.

As someone who works with franchise buyers and sellers every day, I can tell you what separates an A from a C.

An A business runs without the owner in the building. It has a manager who can carry it.

A C business is one where you are the business. Buyers see that as risk, and they pay less for it.

The good news? You can move from C to A before you sell.

Three things to watch:

- Can your business run without you daily?
- Are your books clean and buyer-ready?
- Do you have open territory left to grow?

## VARIANT B — Contrarian Hook

Two fitness businesses can look identical on paper and sell for very different prices.

Same brand. Same unit count. Same revenue.

But one gets a premium and one gets a discount.

Buyers just backed a 34-unit Club Pilates operator, plus a 6-unit and a 3-unit deal.

What they paid attention to wasn't just the numbers. It was the gaps between A, B, and C quality businesses.

Here's what separates them.

An "A" business has room to grow, open territory, and a management team that runs the day-to-day without the owner.

A "C" business leans on the owner for everything.

That gap is widening. Buyers are pickier than they were two years ago.

As someone who works with franchise buyers and sellers every day, this is the part owners miss.

You don't get paid for how hard you work in the business. You get paid for how well it runs without you.

Build a team. Document your systems. Prove you can step away.

Three things to watch:

- Can your business run without you for two weeks?
- Do you have open territory left to grow?
- Is your management bench deep or thin?

# POST 2 — Personal Post

**Topic:** The difference between franchise transfer and franchise resale

---

Most franchise owners think "transfer" and "resale" mean the same thing.

They don't. And that confusion costs people money.

A transfer is the mechanical part. You have a buyer, and now you move the franchise agreement from your name to theirs. The franchisor approves it. Paperwork gets signed.

A resale is the whole deal. Finding the right buyer. Pricing the business. Positioning it so someone actually wants it. The transfer is just the last step.

Here's where owners get stuck.

I worked with an owner who had a buyer lined up on his own. He thought he was done. But he priced the business on gut feeling and left real money on the table. He handled the transfer fine. He never ran a resale.

The transfer protects the franchisor. The resale protects you.

If you only focus on the paperwork, you're skipping the part that determines what you walk away with.

So before you think about moving the agreement, think about what you're actually selling and to whom.

That's the difference between closing a deal and closing a good one.
