The buyer didn't just chase the biggest operator. They picked up small franchisees too.

And they paid attention to three things: stability, room to grow, and a solid management team.

For the small fitness owner, that last point matters most.

Buyers now separate A, B, and C operators. The gap between them is widening.

What moves you up the ladder? A business that runs without you in the room every day.

As someone who works with franchise buyers and sellers every day, I see this play out constantly.

Three things to watch:

- Can your business run without you for 30 days straight?

## VARIANT B — Contrarian Hook

Two fitness studios with the same revenue can sell for wildly different prices.

The gap isn't luck. It's the management team.

A private equity firm just bought into a 34-unit Club Pilates operator. They also picked up a six-unit and a three-unit owner.

Same brand. Different prices.

Buyers used to pay similar money for similar revenue. Not anymore.

Now they dig into who runs the business. Can it grow? Does it run without the owner in the building every day?

For a 1-20 unit owner, this is everything.

If your studio only works because you show up, buyers see risk. Risk means a lower price.

As someone who works with franchise buyers and sellers every day, I'll tell you what most owners miss.

The owner who builds a real team often beats the owner with higher revenue.

Buyers pay for a business that keeps running after you leave. Not a job you happen to own.

Three things to watch:

- Whether your business can run without you in the room

# POST 2 — Personal Post

**Topic:** The one thing that separates a good exit from a great one

---

The best exits aren't about price.

They're about timing.

I've closed over 100 franchise deals in the last 20 years. The owners who walked away happiest weren't always the ones who got the highest number.

They were the ones who sold while the business was still climbing.

Here's what most owners get wrong. They wait until they're tired. Until growth has stalled. Until they "just want out."

By then, buyers can smell it. And your leverage is gone.

I worked with an owner last year who ran four units. Strong margins. Still growing. He could have held on another two years and squeezed out more.

Instead, he sold at the peak. Clean books. Hungry buyer. Multiple offers.

He got a great number and a fast close, because he sold from strength, not exhaustion.

That's the difference.

A good exit happens when you're ready. A great exit happens when the business is ready too.

So don't wait until you're done. Start thinking about it while things are still good.
