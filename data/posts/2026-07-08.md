## VARIANT A — Financial Hook

A private equity firm just bought a 34-unit Club Pilates operator. Plus a 6-unit and a 3-unit deal on top of it.

Here's what caught my eye.

Buyers are getting pickier. They now separate "A" deals from "B" and "C" deals more than ever.

The gap between a clean business and a messy one is growing.

For a fitness or service owner with a handful of units, this matters.

The A and B assets are still trading at strong prices. The C assets are getting left behind.

As someone who works with franchise buyers and sellers every day, the difference is rarely the number of units.

It's whether the business can run without you. A strong second-in-command moves you from a C to an A fast.

Buyers also pay up for "white space" - room to grow, either by opening new locations or buying nearby operators.

Three things to watch:

- Do you have a management team that runs the day-to-day without you?
- Is there open territory a buyer could expand into?
- Are your numbers clean enough to survive a buyer digging in?

## VARIANT B — Contrarian Hook

Two fitness businesses with the same revenue just sold for very different prices.

Same category. Same units. Different multiples.

Why? One had a real management team. The other was the owner doing everything.

That's the gap buyers care about now. They're getting pickier. And they're paying more for businesses that run without the owner in the building.

The old fitness boom is over. But stable concepts with room to grow are still trading strong.

For a 1-20 unit owner, here's what that means.

If your business depends on you, buyers see risk. If it runs on systems and a team, buyers see value.

As someone who works with franchise buyers and sellers every day, I can tell you the "A" deals share three traits.

Steady revenue that doesn't swing month to month.

Room to add locations in the territory.

A team that keeps things running when you're gone.

Miss those and you're a "C" deal. Same units, lower price.

Three things to watch:

- Can your business run without you for two weeks?
- Do you have open territory left to grow into?
- Is your revenue steady or streaky?

# POST 2 — Personal Post

**Topic:** What 20 years of franchise resale transactions has taught me

---

Most owners wait too long to sell.

After 20 years and 100+ transactions, that is the pattern I see over and over.

The best time to sell is when the business is strong. Growing revenue. Clean books. Happy staff. But that is exactly when owners feel least like leaving.

So they wait. Then something changes. A health scare. Burnout. A bad year. And now they are selling from a weaker position.

I had a seller once who ran three units. Great locations. Solid numbers. He held on two extra years because things felt fine. Then a lease issue and a labor crunch hit at the same time. His value dropped by a third before we ever went to market.

Timing is not about your age or how tired you are. It is about the health of the business when a buyer looks at it.

Here is the takeaway. Know your number before you need to sell. Understand what your business is worth today, not just what you hope it will be worth someday.

That knowledge changes every decision you make.
