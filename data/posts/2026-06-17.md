## VARIANT B — Contrarian Hook

Two fitness studios with the same revenue can sell for very different numbers.

Here's why.

A private equity firm just bought into a 34-unit Club Pilates operator. They also picked up a 6-unit and a 3-unit operator at the same time.

But they didn't pay the same price per unit.

Buyers are now splitting businesses into A, B, and C tiers.

The gap between an A deal and a C deal is wider than it's been in years.

What separates them? Stability, room to grow, and a management team that runs without the owner.

For a 1-20 unit owner, this is the whole ballgame.

As someone who works with franchise buyers and sellers every day, I'll tell you what most owners miss.

Revenue gets you in the door. The quality of your business sets the price.

If your studio falls apart when you take a vacation, you're a C asset. No matter how good the top line looks.

Three things to watch:

- Can your business run without you for 30 days?
- Do you have open territory left to grow into?
- Are your same-store sales steady or shaky?

# POST 2 — Personal Post

**Topic:** Why your franchisor relationship matters more than you think in a sale

---

Your buyer isn't the only one who has to say yes.

Your franchisor does too.

This catches sellers off guard all the time. They find a great buyer, agree on a price, and assume the deal is done.

Then the franchisor steps in.

Most franchise agreements give the franchisor approval rights over any sale. They have to approve the buyer. Sometimes they have a right of first refusal. Sometimes there are transfer fees you didn't budget for.

I once worked with an owner who had a clean deal ready to close. The franchisor took two months to approve the buyer, then required them to complete training before the transfer. The buyer almost walked.

A good relationship with your franchisor changes everything here.

The owners who keep their franchisor in the loop early get faster approvals and fewer surprises. The ones who treat it like an afterthought get delays.

So here's the takeaway. Before you sell, read your franchise agreement and call your franchise business consultant. Know the process before you need it.
