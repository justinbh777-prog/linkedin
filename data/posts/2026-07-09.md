## VARIANT A — Financial Hook

A 34-unit Club Pilates operator just took on a private equity partner. So did a 6-unit and a 3-unit owner.

Here's what that tells you.

Fitness valuations have cooled from their post-pandemic highs. But they're stable.

The difference now? Buyers are pickier.

They're separating the strong businesses from the weak ones. And they're paying up for the strong ones.

What makes a business "strong"? Three things: steady revenue, room to grow, and a team that runs the place without the owner.

For a 1-20 unit owner, that last part matters most.

As someone who works with franchise buyers and sellers every day, I can tell you the owner who is the business is the hardest to sell.

If every decision runs through you, a buyer sees risk. That risk lowers your price.

But if you have managers who handle the day-to-day, you have something buyers fight over.

Three things to watch:

- Can your business run for two weeks without you? That's transferability.
- Do you have clear room to add units or revenue? Buyers pay for white space.
- Is your revenue steady and recurring? Memberships beat one-time sales.

## VARIANT B — Contrarian Hook

Two fitness businesses with the same revenue can sell for very different prices.

Here's why.

A private equity firm just backed a 34-unit Club Pilates operator. But they were picky about which deals they'd touch.

Their words: buyers are "digging in more" on the management team, growth rights, and how well the business runs without the owner.

The result? A bigger gap between A-grade and C-grade businesses.

For a 1-20 unit owner, this is the whole ballgame.

A business that can grow and runs without you is an A. A business that needs you at the front desk every day is a C. Same revenue, very different price.

As someone who works with franchise buyers and sellers every day, I see this gap firsthand.

Owners think revenue sets their price. Buyers think risk does.

Every step you take out of the daily grind moves you from a C toward an A. That work takes years, not weeks.

Three things to watch:

- Can your business run without you in the building?
- Do you still have room to open more units?
- Is your team strong enough to stay after you leave?

# POST 2 — Personal Post

**Topic:** What buyers pay a premium for that has nothing to do with revenue

---

The highest offer I ever got a client wasn't for their best-performing location.

It was for the one that could run without them.

Buyers pay a premium for one thing that never shows up on a P&L: how replaceable the owner is.

I've watched two nearly identical franchises go to market the same month. Same revenue. Same brand. Same city.

One owner was the business. He opened, he closed, he knew every customer by name.

The other had a general manager who ran the whole thing while he traveled.

The second one sold for almost 30% more.

Same numbers. Different risk.

Because a buyer isn't just buying your cash flow. They're buying what happens the day after you leave.

If the answer is "everything falls apart," they discount hard. If the answer is "nothing changes," they pay up.

So here's the takeaway. If you're a few years out from selling, start building yourself out of the daily operations now.

That work quietly raises your price more than another good sales month ever will.
