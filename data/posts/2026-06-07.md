$1 billion. For a franchisee — not a franchisor.

Last summer, Bain Capital acquired Sizzling Platter from CapitalSpring — an eight-brand operator group — in a deal valued at ten figures.

That's an unprecedented price tag for PE buying an operator.

And it signals something most franchisees haven't caught up to yet.

What drove the number? Free cash flow generation — and a proven history of growth across multiple brands.

Not the concept. The operator's economics.

Here's the pattern.

PE is no longer just chasing franchisors and royalty streams. They're buying the operators with strong unit economics and durable same-store sales.

The buyer wants free cash flow that scales — and a portfolio that's already been de-risked across multiple brands.

Multi-brand diversification became an asset, not a complication.

As someone who works with franchise buyers and sellers every day, I'll tell you what the headline buried — the deal took 10 months and approval from eight separate franchisors.

That transfer approval gauntlet is where most operator deals die. Eight franchisors, eight sets of interdependent terms, all negotiated simultaneously.

The lesson for owners — your enterprise value is only as real as your transferability. A great EBITDA number means nothing if the franchisor won't approve the buyer.

Three things to watch:

- PE appetite is shifting from franchisor royalty plays to operator-level cash flow.
- Multi-brand portfolios are commanding premium multiples — diversification de-risks the buy.
- Franchisor approval timelines are becoming the real gating factor on enterprise value.
