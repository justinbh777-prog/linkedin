## VARIANT A — Financial Hook

A 34-unit Club Pilates operator just got bought by a private equity firm.

And they didn't stop there. They added a 6-unit and a 3-unit franchisee at the same time.

Here's what that tells you.

Fitness valuations are stable. Down from the pandemic highs, but holding.

But buyers are pickier now. They're sorting deals into A, B, and C piles. The gap between a great business and an average one is widening.

What makes an A asset? Stability. Room to grow. And a management team that runs the place without the owner in the building.

As someone who works with franchise buyers and sellers every day, this is the part most owners miss.

If your business only runs because you're there, buyers see risk. Risk means a lower number.

The owners who get top dollar built a team and a system first. The sale came second.

Three things to watch:

- Can your business run without you for 30 days straight?
- Do you have clear room to grow, or are you tapped out?
- Are your numbers clean enough to survive a buyer's deep dive?

## VARIANT B — Contrarian Hook

Two fitness studios with the same revenue just sold for very different prices.

The difference wasn't the workout. It was the management team.

Investors are getting pickier. They now split deals into A, B, and C buckets.

The gap between the best and the rest is wider than it's been in years.

Here's the kicker: A and B assets are still trading at the same level. So if you're close to an A, it pays to get there.

For a 1-20 unit owner, this matters more than you think.

If your business only runs because you run it, you're a C asset. Buyers discount that hard.

As someone who works with franchise buyers and sellers every day, I'll tell you what moves the needle.

Buyers pay up for stability and room to grow.

They pay even more when a team runs the show without you in the chair every day.

Three things to watch:

- Can your business run without you for a month? That's the A-asset test.
- Do you have white space - territory or units left to grow into?

# POST 2 — Personal Post

**Topic:** Why the best time to think about your exit is right now

---

The best time to sell your business is before you need to.

I learned this the hard way watching owners wait too long.

Most people start thinking about an exit when they are burned out, sick, or done. That is the worst position to negotiate from. Buyers can smell it.

The owners who get the best price? They started planning years before they actually sold.

Here is something I see all the time. An owner finally decides to sell at 60. But their books are messy. They never built a manager who could run things. The whole business depends on them showing up every day.

That business is worth far less than it should be. Not because it makes less money. Because the owner waited until the last minute to think about any of this.

When you plan early, you have time to fix the things that scare buyers away. You build value on purpose.

You do not have to sell now. You just have to know where you stand.

Knowing your number changes how you run your business.
